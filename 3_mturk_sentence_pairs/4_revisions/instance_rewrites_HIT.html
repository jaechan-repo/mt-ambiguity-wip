Your task is to minimally edit a given sentence to fit the description of the sentence. Provided an idiom, one intended ambiguous subsentence, one intended figurative sentence, and one intended literal sentence, you will rewrite them so that they are truly ambiguous, figurative, and literal, respectively.
Definitions of Idiomatic, Literal, and Ambiguous
We use the idiom "piece of cake", with a dictionary definition "something easily achieved", as a running example. If the idiom is used in its idiomatic meaning within the sentence (i.e., used figuratively rather than literally), then the sentence is called idiomatic.

The sentence The boy found the project to be a piece of cake because he planned far ahead is a clear example of an idiomatic use. The piece of cake does not refer to a cake as literal sweet food; it tells how easy the project was for the boy.


If the idiom is used in its literal meaning within the sentence, then the sentence is called literal.

The sentence The boy ate a piece of cake for his dessert is a literal use. The piece of cake refers to an actual food with no hidden meanings behind it.


If it can be interpreted either way (either figuratively or literally), then the sentence is called ambiguous.

The sentence That was a piece of cake is an ambiguous use of the idiom. The sentence might be literally referring to a breadlike food or metaphorically portraying how easy whatever "that" was. Since it can be freely interpreted either way, the sentence is ambiguous.

So, What is the Task?
Given an idiom, one intended ambiguous subsentence (i.e., part of sentence), one intended idiomatic sentence, and one intended literal sentence, you must:
  • Edit the intended ambiguous subsentence so that the subsentence makes use of the provided idiom in an ambiguous way.
  • Edit the intended idiomatic sentence so that the sentence adds additional context to the ambiguous subsentence. After adding this context, the used idiom must no longer be ambiguous; it must only allow for an idiomatic interpretation. This idiomatic sentence must strictly include the ambiguous subsentence (your version if you edited it).
  • Edit the intended literal sentence so that the sentence adds additional context to the ambiguous subsentence. After adding this context, the used idiom must no longer be ambiguous; it must only allow for an literal interpretation. This literal sentence must strictly include the ambiguous subsentence (your version if you edited it).

"Strict inclusion" means that the entirety of the subsentence (word-by-word, character-by-character) must be present in the sentence. Thus, answer to idiomatic and literal sentences will depend on your answer to ambiguous.

The instances that are required to be edited will be marked as ⚠ Required. Completing the required edits is the bare minimum of work needed to submit the work, but you are also responsible for improving the non-required instances. If you get stuck on coming up with good idiomatic and literal sentences because the provided ambiguous is low-quality, feel free to edit the ambiguous in a way you want, even when not marked as required.
Important Example: Piece of Cake
We use the idiom "piece of cake" as a running example to help you understand. Let's take a look at the task:
[Idiom] piece of cake
  • [Def. of Idiom] something easily achieved
Intended Ambiguous
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake
 
Intended Idiomatic
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake because I planned it far ahead.
 
Intended Literal
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake because our team has the most talented worker.
 

In this example, all three of the rewrites are required. Let's tackle the ambiguous subsentence first: "The project is a piece of cake". The subsentence is not truly ambiguous because the subject "The project" already disambiguates the meaning of "the piece of cake". It doesn't make sense for something like a project to be edible, so the subsentence is completely idiomatic, having no room for a literal interpretation. To make it more ambiguous, let's try changing the subject to "it".
Intended Ambiguous
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake
It is a piece of cake

The idiomatic sentence also must be fixed because (1) it is required and (2) even if not required, the idiomatic sentence must strictly include the changed ambiguous subsentence. Let's change the subject to "it" for this one as well:
Intended Idiomatic
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake because I planned it far ahead.
It is a piece of cake because I planned it far ahead.
Note that this change preserves the idiomatic use of the idiom; it is clear that "it" refers to some project or some goal even if not explicitly mentioned. You must always check if your proposed change indeed matches the intended label—which is the whole purpose of this HIT after all.

Finally, let's fix the literal sentence in the same way:
Intended Literal
⚠ Required
The project is a piece of cake because our team has the most talented worker.
It is a piece of cake because our team has the most talented worker.
This is not enough because the sentence is still idiomatic, not literal. A literal interpretation of "piece of cake" would involve actions like baking or eating, rather than having a talented worker. Let's use baking as an example:
The project is a piece of cake because our team has the most talented worker.
It is a piece of cake that Julia and I baked.

That's it. Confirm that your idiomatic and literal sentences strictly include the ambiguous subsentence, and press the submit button. Please read through the other examples as well.
As mentioned previously, you are responsible for completing non-required edits, as well as required ones, for the sake of improving the quality of the subsentences and sentences. Take this example task using the idiom "bright spark":
[Idiom] bright spark
  • [Def. of Idiom] exceptionally intelligent, clever person
Intended Ambiguous
He is the bright spark
 
Intended Idiomatic
He is the bright spark who proposed a groundbreaking mathematical theory at the age of 12!
 
Intended Literal
⚠ Required
He is the bright spark that triggered the dynamites!
 

Let's take a look at the required one: "He is the bright spark that triggered the dynamites". Upon close examination, you will notice that the problem is the personal pronoun "he", which alone disambiguates the meaning of "bright spark" to be a description of a person, rather than a thing that contains light. This means the problem is in the ambiguous subsentence. Let's fix it.
Intended Ambiguous
He is the bright spark
I've never seen such a bright spark
This way, "the bright spark" can either mean a genius person or a physical spark, thus ambiguous in its meaning.

And let's fix the other two instances appropriately so that they strictly include our new ambiguous subsentence:
Intended Idiomatic
He is the bright spark who proposed a groundbreaking mathematical theory at the age of 12!
I've never seen such a bright spark who would propose a groundbreaking mathematical theory at the age of 12!
Intended Literal
⚠ Required
He is the bright spark that triggered the dynamites!
I've never seen such a bright spark caused by lighting up dynamites!

You might face an idiom that makes it nearly impossible to create a literal sentence on top of it. For example, consider the idiom "a bag of nerves":
[Idiom] bag of nerves
  • [Def. of Idiom] nervous person
Intended Ambiguous
I see a bag of nerves
 
Intended Idiomatic
I see a bag of nerves who struggles with interviews.
 
Intended Literal
I see a bag of nerves who performs poorly in front of others.
 

The ambiguous subsentence and idiomatic sentence seem okay, so let's take a look at the intended literal sentence: "I see a bag of nerves who performs poorly in front of others.". We must fix this because the usage of the idiom is idiomatic: a bag of nerves in this sentence figuratively means a nervous person.
Yet upon closer examination, a literal interpretation of "bag of nerves" is not plausible. A backpack contained with a human nervous system? Or a bag that is alive and has senses? Either way, it doesn't seem possible to come up with a good literal sentence using this idiom. In that case, click ❌ Discard button and provide an appropriate reason for discarding this idiom.
