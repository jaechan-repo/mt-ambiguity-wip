Usage of idioms: Literal, Figurative, or Ambiguous?

Thank you for participating in this HIT.

Please read the instructions below completely before proceeding with the task.

Your task is to identify the usage of a given idiom in different sentences.

For each sentence, if the idiom is used in its figurative/idiomatic definition within the sentence, then select figurative. In case you are unfamiliar with the figurative use of the idiom, we provide its dictionary meaning, which you can use as a reference for deciding if the idiom was used figuratively. If the idiom is used in its literal/physical definition within the sentence, then select literal. If it can be interpreted either way (either figuratively or literally), then select ambiguous.

If the usage of the idiom is inherently offensive or not possible to determine among the three choices above, then select invalid. Potential reasons for the invalidness include: the idiom provided is hardly an idiom, the given sentence does not include the idiom, the content of the sentence is incomprehensible, etc.

Take a look at the following case study of idiom "burn the bridge", the figurative definition of which is "to end a relationship":

Choice Select when... Example
figurative The sentence uses the figurative/idiomatic definition of the given idiom. "I burned the bridge with him right after we crossed it"

(Figurative because the notion of burning a bridge idiomatically conveys the idea of ending a relationship with someone)
literal The sentence uses the literal/physical definition of the given idiom. "I burned the bridge with him right after we crossed it"

(Literal because crossing a bridge implies the bridge is a physical structure, not a human relationship)
ambiguous Whether the idiom is being used literally or figuratively is unclear; it can be interpreted either way. "I burned the bridge with him"

(Ambiguous because we can't tell from this sentence whether burning the bridge means ending a relationship or physically burning the structure of a bridge)
